Let me give you an extremely detailed breakdown of how to implement this system properly, covering all the nuances, edge cases, and practical considerations.

## Part 1: Understanding the Data Source (Deribit)

### 1.1 Why Deribit?

Deribit is the dominant Bitcoin options exchange, handling approximately 85-90% of all BTC options volume. This matters because:

- **Liquidity concentration** = tighter bid-ask spreads = more reliable price signals
- Other exchanges (OKX, Binance, CME) have options but with less liquidity
- CME options are cash-settled and have different characteristics (institutional bias)

### 1.2 Deribit API Structure

Deribit's public API requires no authentication for market data. Key endpoints:

```
Base URL: https://www.deribit.com/api/v2/public/

Essential endpoints:
- get_index_price        → Current BTC spot/index price
- get_instruments        → List all available option contracts
- get_book_summary_by_currency → Bulk summary of all options (efficient)
- get_order_book         → Detailed order book for single instrument
```

### 1.3 Understanding Deribit's Instrument Naming Convention

**Format:** `BTC-DDMMMYY-STRIKE-TYPE`

**Examples:**
- `BTC-27DEC24-100000-C` = Bitcoin, Dec 27 2024 expiry, $100,000 strike, Call
- `BTC-28MAR25-50000-P` = Bitcoin, Mar 28 2025 expiry, $50,000 strike, Put

**Critical parsing consideration:** The date format uses 2-digit year and 3-letter month abbreviation. You must handle the month parsing carefully (JAN, FEB, MAR, APR, MAY, JUN, JUL, AUG, SEP, OCT, NOV, DEC).

### 1.4 Deribit Price Quotation (CRITICAL)

**This is where many implementations go wrong.**

Deribit quotes option prices **in BTC, not USD**. 

If you see:
- `bid_price: 0.0150`
- `ask_price: 0.0155`

This means 0.0150 BTC and 0.0155 BTC respectively.

**To convert to USD:** `price_usd = price_btc × spot_price`

If BTC spot is $100,000:
- Bid in USD = 0.0150 × $100,000 = $1,500
- Ask in USD = 0.0155 × $100,000 = $1,550

**Edge case:** During high volatility, the spot price can move significantly between when you fetch the option price and when you fetch the spot. Consider using the `underlying_price` field from the order book if available, or fetch spot first and use consistently.

### 1.5 Data Quality Filters

Before using any option data, apply these filters:

| Filter | Rationale | Suggested Threshold |
|--------|-----------|---------------------|
| Open Interest | Low OI = illiquid = unreliable price | > 10 contracts minimum |
| Bid-Ask Spread | Wide spreads indicate stale/unreliable quotes | < 20% of mid price |
| Both Bid AND Ask exist | One-sided markets are untradeable | Both must be > 0 |
| Time to expiry | Very short-dated options have numerical issues | > 1-2 days |
| Moneyness range | Deep OTM options have near-zero prices | Typically ±50% of spot |

**Edge case - Zero bids:** During market stress, deep OTM options may have zero bids. Exclude these entirely.

**Edge case - Stale quotes:** Deribit may show old quotes for illiquid strikes. Check the `timestamp` field if available.

## Part 2: Converting Prices to Implied Volatility

### 2.1 Why Convert to IV?

Raw option prices are "dirty" - they contain:
- Time value decay
- Interest rate effects  
- Moneyness effects

Implied Volatility normalizes all these effects into a single number representing the market's expectation of future volatility.

### 2.2 The Black-Scholes Model

For European options (which Deribit options are):

**Call Price:**
$$C = S \cdot N(d_1) - K \cdot e^{-rT} \cdot N(d_2)$$

**Put Price:**
$$P = K \cdot e^{-rT} \cdot N(-d_2) - S \cdot N(-d_1)$$

Where:
$$d_1 = \frac{\ln(S/K) + (r + \sigma^2/2)T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}$$
$$d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T}$$

**Variables:**
- $S$ = Spot price (or Forward price if using forward measure)
- $K$ = Strike price
- $T$ = Time to expiry in years
- $r$ = Risk-free rate
- $\sigma$ = Volatility (this is what we're solving for)
- $N(\cdot)$ = Standard normal CDF

### 2.3 Solving for Implied Volatility

There's no closed-form solution for IV. You must use numerical methods.

**Method 1: Brent's Method (Recommended)**
- Bracketed root-finding algorithm
- Guaranteed convergence if solution exists in bracket
- Fast (typically < 10 iterations)
- Search range: typically [0.01, 5.0] for annualized vol (1% to 500%)

**Method 2: Newton-Raphson**
- Uses Vega (derivative of price w.r.t. volatility)
- Can be faster but may diverge
- Needs good initial guess

**Recommended approach:** Use Brent's method with brackets [0.01, 5.0]

### 2.4 Edge Cases in IV Calculation

**Edge Case 1: Arbitrage Violation**

The option price must exceed its intrinsic value:
- Call: Price ≥ max(S - K×e^(-rT), 0)
- Put: Price ≥ max(K×e^(-rT) - S, 0)

If the market price is below intrinsic value, return NaN. This can happen due to:
- Stale quotes
- Data errors
- Extreme market conditions

**Edge Case 2: Deep OTM Options**

Deep out-of-the-money options have prices very close to zero. Small price errors → huge IV errors.

Example: A $200,000 strike call when BTC is at $100,000 might trade at $5. A $1 error (20%) could swing IV by 10+ percentage points.

**Mitigation:** Weight these options less in your SSVI fitting, or exclude options beyond a certain moneyness threshold.

**Edge Case 3: Near-Expiry Options**

As T → 0, the math becomes unstable:
- Division by √T amplifies errors
- Gamma becomes extremely high
- Market makers widen spreads

**Mitigation:** Exclude options with < 2 days to expiry, or handle the T → 0 limit separately.

**Edge Case 4: Very High IV**

Bitcoin can have IVs > 100%, sometimes > 200% during extreme events. Your solver must handle this:
- Extend the search bracket upper bound to 5.0 (500%)
- If still no solution, the price likely violates no-arbitrage bounds

**Edge Case 5: Put-Call Parity Violations**

In theory: C - P = S - K×e^(-rT)

In practice, this may not hold perfectly due to:
- Funding rates
- Collateral costs
- Market microstructure

If you see violations > 2-3%, investigate the data quality.

### 2.5 Choosing Which Options to Use (Calls vs Puts)

**Best practice:** Use the option that is closest to ATM or slightly OTM.

- For strikes K < F (forward): Use **Puts** (these are OTM puts)
- For strikes K > F (forward): Use **Calls** (these are OTM calls)
- For K ≈ F: Use either, or average both

**Rationale:** OTM options are more liquid and have less numerical sensitivity than deep ITM options.

## Part 3: The SSVI Model (Surface Stochastic Volatility Inspired)

### 3.1 Why SSVI?

Raw market IVs are noisy and have gaps. If you try to take derivatives of raw data, you get garbage. SSVI provides:

1. **Smoothness** - Continuous, differentiable surface
2. **Arbitrage-free** - Built-in constraints prevent impossible prices
3. **Parsimonious** - Only 3-4 parameters per expiry slice

### 3.2 The SSVI Parameterization

The model specifies **total implied variance** $w(k, \theta_t)$ as a function of log-moneyness $k$ and ATM total variance $\theta_t$:

$$w(k, \theta_t) = \frac{\theta_t}{2} \left[ 1 + \rho \phi(\theta_t) k + \sqrt{(\phi(\theta_t) k + \rho)^2 + (1-\rho^2)} \right]$$

Where:
- $k = \ln(K/F)$ is log-moneyness (K = strike, F = forward)
- $\theta_t = \sigma_{ATM}^2 \cdot T$ is ATM total variance
- $\rho$ is a correlation parameter (controls skew direction)
- $\phi(\theta)$ is the "wing" function controlling how fast IV increases for OTM strikes

**The Wing Function (Power Law):**
$$\phi(\theta) = \frac{\eta}{\theta^\gamma (1+\theta)^{1-\gamma}}$$

Where:
- $\eta$ = volatility-of-volatility parameter
- $\gamma$ = power-law exponent (0 < γ < 1)

### 3.3 Converting Between Total Variance and IV

$$\text{Total Variance: } w = \sigma^2 \cdot T$$
$$\text{Implied Variance: } \sigma^2 = w / T$$
$$\text{Implied Volatility: } \sigma = \sqrt{w / T}$$

### 3.4 Parameter Constraints (CRITICAL for No-Arbitrage)

**Hard Constraints:**
- $-1 < \rho < 1$ (correlation bounds)
- $\eta > 0$ (positive vol-of-vol)
- $0 < \gamma < 1$ (power law bounds)
- $\theta > 0$ (positive variance)

**Soft Constraint (Butterfly Arbitrage):**
$$\eta (1 + |\rho|) < 2$$

If this condition is violated, the implied density can go negative (which is impossible).

**Calendar Spread Arbitrage:**
The function $\theta_t$ (ATM variance as a function of time) must be increasing. If shorter-dated options have higher ATM variance than longer-dated ones, there's calendar arbitrage.

### 3.5 Fitting the SSVI Model

**Objective Function:**
Minimize weighted sum of squared errors between model IVs and market IVs:

$$\min_{\rho, \eta, \gamma, \theta} \sum_i w_i \left( \sigma_i^{model} - \sigma_i^{market} \right)^2$$

**Weighting Scheme Options:**

| Weight Type | Formula | Rationale |
|-------------|---------|-----------|
| Open Interest | $w_i = \sqrt{OI_i}$ | Liquid options matter more |
| Vega-weighted | $w_i = \text{Vega}_i$ | Errors in high-vega options more important |
| Inverse distance from ATM | $w_i = 1/(1+|k_i|)$ | ATM options most reliable |
| Bid-ask width | $w_i = 1/\text{spread}_i$ | Tight spreads = confident prices |

**Recommended:** Combine OI weighting with inverse distance from ATM.

**Optimization Algorithm:**
- L-BFGS-B (handles box constraints well)
- Or: Differential Evolution for global search, then polish with L-BFGS-B

**Initial Guess Strategy:**
1. Estimate $\theta$ from ATM option's IV squared times T
2. Start $\rho \approx -0.3$ (typical for equity/crypto - downside skew)
3. Start $\eta \approx 0.5$
4. Start $\gamma \approx 0.5$

### 3.6 Edge Cases in SSVI Fitting

**Edge Case 1: Insufficient Data**

If an expiry has < 5 valid options, the fit will be unreliable. Either:
- Skip that expiry
- Use a simpler 2-parameter model (e.g., fixed γ)

**Edge Case 2: No Skew / Flat Smile**

Sometimes the market is pricing almost no skew (ρ ≈ 0). The optimizer might struggle. Add light regularization:

$$\text{Objective} = \text{MSE} + \lambda(\rho^2 + (\eta - 0.5)^2 + (\gamma - 0.5)^2)$$

With λ ≈ 0.001

**Edge Case 3: Extreme Skew**

During crashes or euphoria, skew can be extreme (ρ → ±0.9). Make sure your bounds allow this but check the butterfly condition carefully.

**Edge Case 4: Optimizer Converges to Boundary**

If parameters hit their bounds, the fit may be poor. Consider:
- Widening bounds slightly
- Checking data quality
- Using a different parameterization

**Edge Case 5: Multiple Local Minima**

The SSVI objective can have multiple minima. Try:
- Multiple random restarts
- Global optimization first (differential evolution)
- Warm-starting from a previous expiry's parameters

## Part 4: The Breeden-Litzenberger Formula

### 4.1 The Core Theorem

Published in 1978, this remarkable result states:

$$f(K) = e^{rT} \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial K^2}$$

Where:
- $f(K)$ = risk-neutral probability density at price $K$
- $C$ = call option price as a function of strike $K$
- $r$ = risk-free rate
- $T$ = time to expiry

**Intuition:** The second derivative captures the "curvature" of option prices. Where prices curve more sharply, the market is assigning higher probability.

### 4.2 Mathematical Derivation (Brief)

The price of a call option is:
$$C(K) = e^{-rT} \int_K^\infty (S_T - K) f(S_T) dS_T$$

Taking the first derivative w.r.t. K:
$$\frac{\partial C}{\partial K} = -e^{-rT} \int_K^\infty f(S_T) dS_T = -e^{-rT}[1 - F(K)]$$

Where F(K) is the CDF. Taking the second derivative:
$$\frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial K^2} = e^{-rT} f(K)$$

Rearranging gives the Breeden-Litzenberger formula.

### 4.3 Numerical Implementation

Since we have a smooth SSVI surface, we compute the second derivative numerically using **central differences**:

$$\frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial K^2} \approx \frac{C(K+h) - 2C(K) + C(K-h)}{h^2}$$

**Step size h:** This is crucial.
- Too small: Numerical noise dominates
- Too large: Poor approximation of derivative

**Recommended:** h = 0.5% to 1% of the strike price

For a $100,000 strike: h ≈ $500 to $1,000

### 4.4 Full Calculation Pipeline

For each strike K you want to evaluate:

1. **Calculate log-moneyness:** $k = \ln(K/F)$
2. **Get total variance from SSVI:** $w = \text{SSVI}(k, \theta)$
3. **Convert to IV:** $\sigma = \sqrt{w/T}$
4. **Price the call using Black-Scholes:** $C = BS_{call}(S, K, T, r, \sigma)$
5. **Repeat for K-h and K+h**
6. **Apply central difference formula**
7. **Apply Breeden-Litzenberger:** $f(K) = e^{rT} \times \text{second derivative}$

### 4.5 Edge Cases in RND Extraction

**Edge Case 1: Negative Density**

If your SSVI fit violates no-arbitrage conditions, the second derivative can be negative, implying negative probability. This is impossible.

**Mitigation:** 
- Enforce butterfly arbitrage constraint in SSVI fitting
- Floor the density at zero: $f(K) = \max(f(K), 0)$
- If widespread negatives, reject the fit and investigate

**Edge Case 2: Density Doesn't Integrate to 1**

Due to:
- Truncation (you're not computing from 0 to ∞)
- Numerical errors
- Model limitations

**Mitigation:** Normalize the density:
$$f_{normalized}(K) = \frac{f(K)}{\int_{K_{min}}^{K_{max}} f(K) dK}$$

Use numerical integration (trapezoidal rule is fine).

**Edge Case 3: Tails Are Poorly Defined**

Far OTM options are illiquid, so the SSVI fit may be unreliable in the tails.

**Mitigation:**
- Don't extrapolate too far beyond the data
- Consider fitting a log-normal tail extension
- Report confidence only within a reasonable range (e.g., 5th to 95th percentile)

**Edge Case 4: Step Size Sensitivity**

Results shouldn't change dramatically with different h values.

**Validation:** Compute density with h, h/2, and 2h. If results differ by > 5%, your surface may not be smooth enough.

## Part 5: Interpreting the Results

### 5.1 Key Statistics to Extract

From the density f(K), compute:

| Statistic | Formula | Interpretation |
|-----------|---------|----------------|
| Mean | $E[S_T] = \int K \cdot f(K) dK$ | Expected price (risk-neutral) |
| Mode | $\arg\max_K f(K)$ | Most likely price |
| Variance | $\int (K - \mu)^2 f(K) dK$ | Uncertainty |
| Skewness | $\frac{\int (K-\mu)^3 f(K) dK}{\sigma^3}$ | Asymmetry of distribution |
| Kurtosis | $\frac{\int (K-\mu)^4 f(K) dK}{\sigma^4} - 3$ | Tail fatness (excess over normal) |
| Percentiles | $K$ such that $\int_0^K f(x)dx = p$ | Probability thresholds |

### 5.2 Interpreting Skewness

| Skewness | Interpretation |
|----------|---------------|
| Negative (e.g., -0.3) | Left tail is fatter. Market is paying more for crash protection. Puts are "expensive" relative to calls. |
| Near Zero | Symmetric distribution. Market sees equal upside/downside risk. |
| Positive (e.g., +0.3) | Right tail is fatter. Market is paying more for upside exposure. Calls are relatively expensive. |

**Typical crypto pattern:** Negative skew (crash fear) during uncertain times, can flip to positive during euphoric rallies.

### 5.3 Interpreting Kurtosis

| Excess Kurtosis | Interpretation |
|-----------------|----------------|
| Positive (> 0.5) | Fatter tails than normal. Market expects more extreme moves (both up and down). |
| Near Zero | Similar to normal distribution. |
| Negative (rare) | Thinner tails. Market expects prices to stay in a narrow range. |

**Crypto typically shows high positive kurtosis** - the market expects big moves.

### 5.4 The Risk-Neutral vs Real-World Distinction (CRITICAL)

**These probabilities are NOT predictions!**

They are **risk-neutral** probabilities, which incorporate:
- True probability beliefs
- Risk premiums (what investors are willing to pay for protection)

**Example:** 
- True probability of a 20% crash in 30 days: 5%
- Risk-neutral probability from options: 12%

Why the difference? Investors are **risk-averse** and pay extra for crash protection. The 12% includes a "fear premium."

**Implication:** The RND overstates tail risks that people want to hedge against. It's a measure of *market fear* as much as *market belief*.

### 5.5 Comparing Across Expiries (Term Structure)

How do statistics change with time to expiry?

| Pattern | Interpretation |
|---------|----------------|
| Skew becomes more negative for longer expiries | Long-term crash fear exceeds short-term |
| Variance scales linearly with T | Consistent with random walk |
| Variance scales faster than T | Market expects volatility clustering |
| Mode diverges from forward | Directional market bias |

## Part 6: Practical Implementation Considerations

### 6.1 Data Refresh Frequency

Options data changes continuously. Consider:

| Use Case | Refresh Frequency |
|----------|-------------------|
| End-of-day analysis | Once daily (after 00:00 UTC settlement) |
| Intraday monitoring | Every 5-15 minutes |
| Real-time trading | Stream via WebSocket |

### 6.2 Handling Multiple Expiries

**Option 1: Fit each expiry independently**
- Simpler
- No cross-expiry constraints
- May have inconsistent parameters across expiries

**Option 2: Joint calibration (advanced)**
- Fit all expiries simultaneously
- Enforce calendar spread no-arbitrage
- More complex, but more robust

**Recommendation:** Start with independent fits, check for calendar arbitrage violations manually.

### 6.3 Computational Performance

For a single expiry with 50 options:
- IV calculations: ~50 Brent solves → < 100ms
- SSVI fitting: ~100-1000 optimizer iterations → 100ms - 1s
- RND extraction: ~500 Black-Scholes calls → < 100ms

Total: Should complete in 1-2 seconds per expiry.

**Optimization tips:**
- Vectorize Black-Scholes calculations with NumPy
- Cache intermediate calculations
- Use JAX/Numba for hot loops if needed

### 6.4 Error Handling Checklist

| Error Type | Detection | Response |
|------------|-----------|----------|
| API timeout | requests.Timeout | Retry with exponential backoff |
| No data for expiry | Empty options list | Skip expiry, log warning |
| IV solver failure | NaN returned | Exclude that option |
| SSVI fit poor (high MSE) | Track fit quality | Flag for manual review |
| Negative densities | f(K) < 0 | Check butterfly condition, possibly reject fit |
| Density doesn't sum to 1 | ∫f(K) ≠ 1 | Normalize |

### 6.5 Validation Strategies

**1. Put-Call Parity Check**
Verify C - P ≈ F - K×e^(-rT) for liquid ATM options

**2. IV Smile Sanity**
ATM IV should be in reasonable range (typically 40-120% for BTC)
IV should increase for deep OTM options (smile/smirk shape)

**3. Cross-Expiry Consistency**
Longer expiries should generally have higher total variance

**4. Backtest Against Realized Outcomes**
After expiry, compare the realized price to the predicted distribution
Over many samples, check if the percentiles are calibrated

## Part 9: Common Pitfalls to Avoid

1. **Forgetting BTC-denomination:** Deribit prices are in BTC!

2. **Using calendar days instead of trading days:** For crypto (24/7), use calendar days. For traditional markets, use trading days.

3. **Ignoring the forward price:** Use F = S×e^(rT), not just spot S

4. **Taking derivatives of raw data:** Always smooth first (SSVI or interpolation)

5. **Extrapolating too far:** Don't trust the model beyond the data range

6. **Misinterpreting risk-neutral as real-world:** These include risk premiums!

7. **Using mid prices blindly:** In illiquid options, the mid can be misleading

8. **Not checking no-arbitrage conditions:** Can produce nonsensical results
